# Why Ed25519?

A plain-language explainer of why EVP/1 verdicts are signed with Ed25519.

## The problem

A verdict that can be silently modified after issuance has no provenance. If you receive a JSON object claiming to be an Etymolt verdict saying "PROCEED on the name 'Acme'," how do you know:

1. Etymolt actually issued it?
2. Nobody altered the score, the axes, or the disclaimer between issuance and your read?
3. The verdict you're acting on is the same verdict an audit might surface later?

Without signatures, you don't. With signatures, you do.

## Why Ed25519 specifically

Ed25519 is the elliptic-curve signature scheme defined in [RFC 8032](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8032). It's the default in modern protocols (TLS 1.3, SSH, sigstore, in-toto, JWS in newer applications). Compared to alternatives:

- **vs. RSA:** smaller keys (32-byte public keys vs. 256+), faster verification, no per-message key-generation risk.
- **vs. ECDSA P-256:** no nonce reuse failures (Ed25519 is deterministic), smaller signatures (64 bytes), faster.
- **vs. HMAC:** asymmetric — you can verify without knowing the secret. HMAC requires shared secrets, which doesn't work for the multi-consumer trust graph EVP/1 needs.

The pragmatic argument: Ed25519 is what every modern signing protocol uses in 2026. Picking anything else would require justification.

## Why detached signatures

The signature is a separate field on the verdict (`signature` + `signature_key_id` + `signature_payload_digest`) rather than embedded in or replacing the payload. This means:

- The verdict JSON remains human-readable and machine-parseable without signature-stripping logic.
- The signature can be regenerated under a new key without rewriting the payload.
- The same payload can carry multiple signatures during key-rotation overlap windows (see [EVP-0004](../evps/EVP-0004-key-rotation.md)).

## Why JCS canonicalization

JSON is a flexible serialization format. The same logical object can be serialized differently — different key orders, different whitespace, different number representations. A signature over one serialization wouldn't verify against another.

[RFC 8785 JCS (JSON Canonicalization Scheme)](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc8785) is a deterministic JSON serialization: keys are sorted lexicographically, numbers follow a specific format, strings are normalized to UTF-8 without escape sequences where avoidable. The signature is computed over the JCS form. Any consumer can re-canonicalize and re-verify.

## What signatures don't protect against

- **The verdict being wrong.** Signing guarantees authenticity, not accuracy. If Etymolt's USPTO ingest was stale at the moment of issuance, the verdict is still authentically Etymolt's — and still wrong about the registry. Cross-checked accuracy is a separate problem solved by transparency logs, multi-issuer corroboration, and audit (out of scope for 1.0).
- **A consumer ignoring the disclaimer.** Signing the disclaimer prevents tampering. It doesn't prevent a sloppy consumer from rendering only the score and dropping the disclaimer field. The verbatim-rendering requirement (see [EVP-0003](../evps/EVP-0003-bureau-model-disclaimer.md)) is the soft enforcement.
- **A compromised signing key.** This is what key rotation (see [EVP-0004](../evps/EVP-0004-key-rotation.md)) and revocation lists exist for.

## Key publication

Etymolt's active signing keys live at `https://api.etymolt.com/.well-known/evp-keys.json`. The revocation list lives at `https://api.etymolt.com/.well-known/evp-keys-revoked.json`. Both follow a JWKS-style structure (RFC 7517 informational).

Future third-party issuers publish their own key directories at parallel well-known URLs under their own domain. The protocol does not require a centralized trust root — each issuer is its own root.
